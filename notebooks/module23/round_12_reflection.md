# Reflection: PCA Lens on BBO Optimisation (Round 12, 22 Data Points)

## 1. How has your optimisation strategy evolved since your first few rounds?

My strategy has undergone three distinct phases. Rounds 1-4 were exploratory: random Latin hypercube sampling to map each function's landscape. Rounds 5-8 shifted to GP-based Bayesian optimisation, narrowing around promising regions but still relying on broad global candidate pools. Rounds 9-12 represent a structurally informed approach. In round 10, I introduced boundary exploration based on raw correlation analysis; in round 11, K-Means clustering identified basins of attraction; now in round 12, PCA provides a principled way to identify which directions in input space actually drive performance. The most systematic element is the use of constrained differential evolution bounds derived directly from PCA loadings and raw correlations: for F4 (5D), the first principal component (r = -0.962, p < 0.001) aligns almost perfectly with the [1,0,1,0,1] alternating corner pattern, so I constrained the DE search to [0.85,1] x [0,0.15] x [0.85,1] x [0,0.15] x [0.8,1]. This represents a genuine transition from "search everywhere" to "search where the data tells you to look."

## 2. Which variables or behaviours drive the largest variation in results?

PCA reveals remarkably clear principal directions for several functions. **F4** is the most dramatic: PC0 explains 67% of query variance and correlates at r = -0.962 with output. Its loadings (x3: +0.52, x2: -0.45, x5: +0.45) map directly onto the "odd dimensions high, even dimensions low" pattern. For **F3**, PC0 (57% variance, r = -0.882) has loadings showing that the x1/x2 versus x3 contrast drives almost all performance variation -- low x1 and x2 with high x3 consistently produce the best outputs. **F5** is the most instructive case: PC1 (r = +0.820) reveals that high x1 and x3 are the principal drivers of catastrophically high outputs (R11's boundary query at x1=1, x3=1 scored 3731), while x5 and x6 contribute little variance. For **F7**, PC1 (r = +0.795) identifies x8 as the single strongest driver (loading +0.57), consistent with the raw correlation of r = +0.923, meaning low x8 is essential. In PCA terms, the "first principal component of performance" is interpretable for most functions, and 2-3 components typically capture over 90% of the relevant structure.

## 3. How do you decide what to keep exploring versus what to simplify?

The decision mirrors PCA's eigenvalue threshold: I "retain" dimensions that show statistically significant correlations (p < 0.05) with output and "reduce" those that don't. For F4, x1/x2/x3/x4/x5 all have |r| > 0.6, so all five dimensions remain active but tightly constrained around the known-good corner. For F5, x2 (r = +0.10) and x6 (r = -0.007) are effectively "zero-variance components" -- they contribute little to output variation, so I let them float within moderate bounds while aggressively constraining x1 and x3 (the high-variance dimensions). For F6, the strategy retains full exploration around the elusive R2 basin because no round since R2 has beaten it, suggesting the true optimum lies in a narrow, high-x2/high-x5/high-x6 region that my GP hasn't adequately modelled. The practical rule: if a dimension's correlation with output exceeds |0.5|, constrain it toward the favourable direction; if it's below |0.2|, let it explore freely; in between, use the GP to decide.

## 4. How might this round influence your final round in Module 24?

Round 12 provides critical information for the final round. If F3's query (x4 = 1.0 with the low-x1/x2 basin) improves on R11's -0.179, it confirms the x4 dimension has a secondary basin to exploit. If F4's slightly pulled-back corner [0.85, 0, 1, 0.15, 0.8] performs worse than [1, 0, 1, 0, 1], it confirms the exact corner is optimal and round 13 should push even more precisely to the boundary. For F5, the constrained interior query near R9 should validate whether the low-x1/x3 pattern is robust. Round 13's strategy will be almost entirely exploitative: for each function, I'll either fine-tune the confirmed best basin (exploitation) or, for functions like F6 where the best hasn't improved in 10 rounds, attempt one final high-variance exploration along the PCA direction most correlated with low output. The balance will shift to roughly 90% exploitation, 10% exploration.

## 5. How do PCA insights apply to interpreting BBO results?

PCA's core principles -- focusing on variance, removing redundancy, and identifying dominant directions -- map directly onto BBO strategy. Just as PCA ranks components by explained variance, my analysis ranks input dimensions by their correlation with output, focusing computational effort on the "high-eigenvalue" dimensions (x3 for F4, x8 for F7) while treating low-correlation dimensions as noise. The concept of cumulative explained variance translates to the observation that for most functions, 2-3 constrained dimensions capture the essential structure. PCA's removal of redundancy parallels my elimination of boundary exploration for F5 after two catastrophic results: that entire region of the search space is "redundant" in the sense that it provides no useful information about the minimum. Perhaps most importantly, PCA's assumption that linear combinations capture the main structure guided my constrained DE bounds -- by projecting the search along PCA-identified directions, I reduce a 9-dimensional optimisation (F8) to an effectively 3-4 dimensional one, just as PCA would project high-dimensional data onto its top few components while retaining most of the information.
